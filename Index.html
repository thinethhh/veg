Welcome to Sri Lanka
turtle hatcheries

Sri Lanka is a haven for globally endangered species, including sea turtles. The country takes pride in its diverse turtle population, which includes Olive Ridley, Green, Hawksbill, and Leatherback turtles. Along the western and southern coasts, sea turtle hatcheries provide a safe haven for turtle eggs and injured turtles, playing a vital role in their rehabilitation and protection. These hatcheries diligently collect and safeguard the eggs until they are ready to hatch and make their journey back to the ocean. Sri Lanka's unwavering dedication to turtle conservation is evident through its conservation efforts and establishment of protected zones, ensuring the long-term survival of these majestic creatures. Visitors to the hatcheries have the unique opportunity to witness the mesmerizing nesting process of female sea turtles and actively participate in the gratifying experience of releasing hatchlings into the vast ocean. Through these collective initiatives, Sri Lanka has adopted a comprehensive and purposeful approach to turtle conservation, fostering a thriving turtle population.

RECENT NEWS


Rare Leatherback turtle hatched at Matara turtle hatchery
  1. This is the world’s largest turtle species and rarely appear in down south beaches.
  2. This turtle’s birth is significant to the country as it is a rare occurrence.
  3. About 80 eggs were collected along the beaches in Matara two months ago and placed at the hatchery.
Navy releases 146 sea turtle hatchlings into ocean in panama beach
  1. The hatchlings were part of the Conservation Project at Panama beach and their eggs were conserved at the Turtle Conservation Unit in Mahanaga.
  2. Rear Admiral Kanchana Banagoda of the Commander Southeastern Naval Area was present at the release.
Sri Lankan Coast guards releases baby turtles in benthota
  1. The Coast Guard Turtle Conservation Projects released 1603 turtle hatchlings from 1903 conserved eggs across the island.
  2. The Sri Lanka Coast Guard (SLCG) safely released the hatchlings into the sea, emphasizing their ongoing efforts to protect turtle eggs.
