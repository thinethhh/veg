Conserving Turtle Habitats
Addressing Threats

01. FISHERIES BYCATCH


The fishing industry is responsible for the deaths of thousands to tens of thousands of sea turtles each year. These magnificent creatures often get ensnared in longlines, gill nets, and trawls, being discarded as bycatch. Even those fortunate enough to escape fishing gear face adverse consequences. The disruption to their food supply and habitat leaves them vulnerable to further harm. It is a pressing concern that necessitates immediate attention and concerted efforts to mitigate these detrimental impacts on sea turtle populations.

CONSEQUENCES


  • Fishing gear causes high sea turtle mortality, endangering species like loggerheads, green turtles, and leatherbacks.
  • Gear damage disrupts sea turtle habitats, reducing foraging and nesting areas.
  • Endangered sea turtles face extinction risks from fishing gear, demanding sustainable practices.

CONSERVATION METHODS


  • World Wildlife Foundation supports new fishing gear to reduce threats to sea turtles.
  • World Wildlife Foundation raises awareness and promotes responsible fishing practices to protect sea turtles.

02. COASTAL DEVELOPMENT


Each year, the habitats of sea turtles suffer significant destruction due to the reduction of coastlines. The presence of boat vessel traffic, the construction of new hotels or high-rise buildings along the shore, and activities such as sea floor dredging and beach erosion greatly impact the availability of food supplies and nesting areas for sea turtles. Consequently, these factors contribute to the decline of sea turtle habitats and the challenges they face in finding suitable places to feed and reproduce.

CONSEQUENCES


  • Construction pollutes water near shore, endangering sea turtle mating habitats.
  • Coastal development hinders female turtles' nesting and hatchlings' journey.
  • Artificial lights from construction confuse hatchlings, leading them astray.
  • Construction worsens vulnerability, contributing to declining sea turtle populations.

CONSERVATION METHODS


  • Coastal setbacks implemented to protect sea turtle habitats during development.
  • Lighting regulations enacted to minimize light pollution and prevent hatchling disorientation.
  • Conservation and restoration efforts focus on preserving and restoring sea turtle habitats affected by coastal development.

03. POLUTION AND PATHOGENS


Sea turtles are adversely affected by marine pollution in various ways. They encounter harm from plastic pollution, discarded fishing gear, petroleum by-products, and debris as they ingest or become entangled in them. Additionally, ocean pollution has detrimental effects on the turtles' immune systems, disrupts their nesting behavior, and hampers the orientation of hatchlings. The presence of these pollutants and contaminants poses significant threats to the health, survival, and natural behaviors of sea turtles, highlighting the urgent need for measures to address and mitigate marine pollution to safeguard their well-being.

CONSEQUENCES


  • Ocean pollution harms sea turtles at all life stages, causing ingestion of toxins and weakening their immune systems.
  • Tar balls formed from degraded oil are a major threat to sea turtles, with many hatchlings ingesting them
  • Chronic exposure to pollutants increases sea turtles' susceptibility to disease, mortality, predation, and boat collisions.

CONSERVATION METHODS


  • Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) safeguard sea turtle habitats from pollution and pathogens.
  • Best Management Practices (BMPs) minimize pollution and protect turtles in fishing, shipping, and tourism industries.
  • Public awareness and education programs promote responsible actions to reduce pollution and protect sea turtles.

04. DIRECT TAKE


Turtles worldwide face a grim fate as they are hunted and sold internationally for their shells, meat, oil, leather, and jewelry. The hawkbill turtle, in particular, has suffered immensely over the last century, with millions of them killed solely for the value of their shells. Despite conservation initiatives making progress in curbing the global trade of luxury and artisanal goods, turtles in Africa, Asia, and the Americas continue to face persistent threats from this industry.

CONSEQUENCES


  • Hawksbill sea turtles, with prized shells, are critically endangered due to hunting. They face threats in the Indian/Pacific oceans and the Caribbean.
  • Hawksbill populations have declined 90% in a century, highlighting the urgent need for conservation.
  • Other sea turtle species are slaughtered for their skin to produce leather goods.

CONSERVATION METHODS


  • Strengthened law enforcement combats illegal shell trade, increasing patrols and collaborating with international agencies.
  • Public awareness and education campaigns discourage participation in the illegal trade, informing communities about sea turtle conservation and legal protections.
  • International cooperation and regulations, such as CITES, control the import/export of sea turtle shells, facilitating collaboration and monitoring to combat illegal trade..

05. CLIMATE CHANGE


Our understanding of the impact of climate change on sea turtles is growing. Climate change has the potential to disrupt the natural sex ratios of hatchlings, increase the vulnerability to disease outbreaks, and intensify the occurrence of extreme weather events. These events, including storms and rising sea levels, pose a significant threat as they destroy vital nesting beaches and fragile coral reefs. Recognizing the intricate relationship between climate change and sea turtles is crucial for implementing effective conservation strategies and mitigating the adverse effects on their populations and habitats.

CONSEQUENCES


  • Rising sea levels and stronger storms destroy sea turtle beach habitats.
  • Warming oceans disrupt ocean currents, exposing turtles to new predators and harming coral reefs vital for their survival.
  • Climate change-induced temperature changes in nesting beaches may lead to a shortage of male sea turtles, endangering their reproductive success and pushing them closer to extinction.

CONSERVATION METHODS


  • Habitat conservation and restoration efforts protect sea turtle habitats from climatic changes.
  • Adaptation strategies are implemented to address the challenges of climate change, such as relocating nests and regulating sand temperature.
  • Collaborative research and monitoring inform conservation management and policy development for sea turtles in the face of climate change.
