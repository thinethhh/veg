Exploring turtle
species diversity

Out of the 250 turtle species, seven are sea turtles living in the ocean, while the rest are terrestrial turtles or live in freshwater. Sri Lankan shores are visited by five sea turtle species: Green Turtle, Hawksbill Turtle, Olive Ridley, Loggerhead, and Leatherback. Sri Lanka also has three species of freshwater turtles and one terrestrial tortoise called the Star tortoise. Male sea turtles never return to land once they enter the sea as hatchlings, while females return to the same beach to nest and lay eggs after mating at sea.

The number of eggs laid varies among turtle species. After laying eggs and covering them with sand, the female returns to the sea. The trail left by her flippers helps predators and humans locate the nest. Only one or two hatchlings out of the numerous eggs survive to adulthood. Adult sea turtles are preyed upon by Great White Sharks, Tiger Sharks, and Orcas. According to the WWF, Olive Ridley is vulnerable, Loggerhead and Green are endangered, and Leatherback and Hawksbill are critically endangered among the sea turtle species nesting in Sri Lanka.

TURTLE CATEGORIES


GREEN TURTLE


The green turtle is one of the largest sea turtles and the only herbivore among the different species. Green turtles are in fact named for the greenish color of their cartilage and fat, not their shells. In the Eastern Pacific, a group of green turtles that have darker shells are called black turtles by the local community. Green turtles are found mainly in tropical and subtropical waters. Like other sea turtles, they migrate long distances between feeding grounds and the beaches from where they hatched. Classified as endangered, green turtles are threatened by overharvesting of their eggs, hunting of adults, being caught in fishing gear and loss of nesting beach sites.


SIGNIFICANCE OF GREEN TURTLES


  • Green turtles graze on seagrasses, maintaining their productivity and health.
  • Digestion of seagrass by green turtles releases recycled nutrients, benefiting the seagrass ecosystem.
  • Seagrass beds serve as vital nurseries for commercially valuable invertebrates and fish.
  • Preserving green turtles ensures the stability of seagrass ecosystems.
  • Conservation of seagrass habitats, supported by green turtles, contributes to long-term food security.

HAWKSBILL TURTLE


Hawksbill turtles are named for their pointed beak and distinctive shell pattern with serrated edges. Their shells are highly prized and sold as "tortoiseshell" due to their colorful appearance. These turtles primarily inhabit tropical oceans, particularly coral reefs. They feed on sponges, extracting them from reef crevices using their narrow beaks, but also consume sea anemones and jellyfish. Sea turtles have been present in Earth's seas for 100 million years, playing a vital role in marine ecosystems by contributing to the health of coral reefs and seagrass beds.


SIGNIFICANCE OF HAWKSBILL TURTLES


  • Hawksbill turtles benefit coral reefs by removing prey, improving access for reef fish to feed.
  • Hawksbill turtles have cultural and tourism value, providing important income for local communities.
  • Tourism driven by hawksbill turtles promotes sustainable practices and environmental conservation.
  • Hawksbill turtles enhance biodiversity and ecological resilience in coral reef areas.

OLIVE RIDLEY TURTLE


The Olive Ridley sea turtle derives its name from the olive green color of its shell. Despite being the most abundant among sea turtles, their vulnerable status arises from their nesting habits in a limited number of locations. Even a disturbance to a single nest beach can have significant repercussions on the entire population. This highlights the importance of protecting and preserving their nesting sites to ensure the long-term survival of Olive Ridley sea turtles and maintain their population stability.


SIGNIFICANCE OF OLIVE RIDLEY TURTLES


  • Olive Ridley turtles have an olive green shell and are abundant globally.
  • They nest in arribadas, where numerous females come ashore to lay eggs simultaneously.
  • Habitat loss, pollution, entanglement, and poaching pose threats to Olive Ridley turtles.
  • Conservation efforts are vital for their survival.

LOGGERHEAD TURTLE


Loggerhead turtles, named for their large heads and powerful jaws, can crush hard-shelled prey. They face less hunting pressure compared to other sea turtles but are significantly affected by bycatch. Loggerheads frequently encounter fisheries and are unintentionally captured in fishing gear. Sea turtles, existing for millions of years, play a crucial role in marine ecosystems by maintaining the health of coral reefs and seagrass beds. They are fundamental links in the marine ecosystem, ensuring its balance and vitality.


SIGNIFICANCE OF LOGGERHEAD TURTLES


  • Loggerhead turtles recycle nutrients that they consume and balance ocean floor sediments by feeding on hard-shelled prey.
  • Their shells provide important habitat for numerous plant and animal species.
  • A single loggerhead turtle can host up to 100 different species on its shell.
  • This interdependence highlights the crucial role of loggerhead turtles in marine ecosystems.

LEATHERBACK TURTLE


Leatherback turtles, the largest sea turtle species, undertake remarkable migrations. Their wide distribution, although impacted by egg collection and fisheries bycatch, has seen a significant decline over the past century. Globally, leatherbacks are classified as Vulnerable by the IUCN, with specific subpopulations considered Critically Endangered. These turtles play a crucial role in marine ecosystems, and their conservation is vital for the preservation of their species and the overall health of our oceans.


SIGNIFICANCE OF LEATHERBACK TURTLES


  • Leatherback turtles help control jellyfish populations by consuming them in significant amounts
  • Leatherbacks attract ecotourism, providing valuable income for coastal communities.
  • They have downward curving spines in their throat which help them to capture their prey.
  • They are considered Vulnerable around the world by the IUCN Red List and listed as Endangered
